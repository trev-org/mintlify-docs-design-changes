**Quick Resume** lets Xbox Series X|S players hold the state of multiple titles in memory at once and jump between them in seconds. Your game looks and feels like it never left memory — because it didn't. As a developer, your job is to make sure the world the player returns to is still valid.

## What Quick Resume actually does

Quick Resume is the Xbox platform's process lifecycle management (PLM) at scale. When the player switches away from your title, the OS:

1. Sends your process a **suspending** notification.
2. Stops scheduling your threads.
3. Snapshots your virtual memory and GPU resources to fast NVMe.
4. Frees the foreground for another title.

When the player returns, the OS rehydrates the snapshot, resumes your threads, and sends a **resuming** notification. Open file handles, network connections (TCP/UDP), GPU command queues, and async tasks all survive in place.

## What does _not_ survive

Lots of state is logically stale even though it is still in memory. You are responsible for refreshing it on resume:

- **Clock and elapsed time** — hours or days may have passed. Re-query `XGetTickCount64`, system time, and any wall-clock timer.
- **Live service state** — friends, presence, party, multiplayer session, sign-in token, store catalog. Tokens may be expired; refresh them.
- **Network reachability** — the player may have changed networks, lost connectivity, or switched continents. Re-validate before you assume your last socket is still useful.
- **Controller bindings** — the gamepad set or active user may have changed; re-query through XUser and GameInput.
- **Title updates and DLC** — a content update may have shipped while you were suspended; check on resume and prompt for re-launch if needed.
- **Date/time-sensitive content** — daily quests, live events, leaderboards, season passes.

## Handling suspend and resume

<Steps>
  <Step title="Register for PLM notifications">
    Subscribe to `CoreApplication::Suspending` (or the GameRuntime equivalent) on startup. Keep the handler fast — you have roughly two seconds before the OS stops scheduling you.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Persist anything that must survive a cold start">
    A Quick Resume snapshot lives in memory; a power-cycle wipes it. Treat the suspend handler as your last chance to flush save data, network checkpoints, and analytics events to disk or cloud.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Detach from the GPU cleanly">
    Wait for outstanding command lists with `ID3D12CommandQueue::Wait`, close any work you don't want resumed, and call `Trim()` on the device so the driver can repack VRAM in the snapshot.
  </Step>
  <Step title="On resume, validate world state">
    In the resuming callback, refresh tokens, query elapsed time, re-validate the live multiplayer session, and check for content updates before unpausing gameplay.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Show a quick re-orient UI if needed">
    If the resume took a meaningful amount of wall-clock time (player was away for hours), briefly pause and tell them what just happened — "You were away from the match for 4 hours; rejoining lobby."
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Network sessions and multiplayer

Long-lived TCP/UDP sockets technically survive the snapshot, but the remote peer almost certainly does not still think you are connected. Treat every multiplayer session as **invalid** on resume:

- Drop and reopen sockets.
- Re-join the MPSD session, lobby, or match through your matchmaking service.
- Reconcile any local game state with authoritative server state before resuming gameplay.

## Friends, presence, and store

XboxLive tokens, friend lists, presence records, and store catalogs all need a refresh. Don't display stale friend status or stale prices.

## Quick Resume and DirectStorage

DirectStorage requests in flight at suspend time are paused and replayed on resume. File handles passed to `IDStorage*` are preserved across the snapshot. You do not need to recreate `IDStorageFile` objects.

## Testing Quick Resume

Dev kits and retail consoles in dev mode let you trigger suspend/resume from the dashboard. Test:

- Long suspend across an Xbox system update.
- Suspend during a multiplayer match.
- Suspend during a long load screen, DirectStorage stream, or async save.
- Suspend with the player signed out, then resumed after a different user signs in.

A correct Quick Resume implementation is invisible to the player. A wrong one ships stale leaderboards, dead lobbies, and "why is my clock wrong?" reports.
